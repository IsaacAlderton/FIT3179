Global CO2 Emissions and Population Statistics

One of the major contributing factors to climate change is the creation of CO2 emissons from fossil fuels. This visualisation explores trends of CO2 emission production and population growth on a global scale.

The countries with the highest CO2 emissions per capita in 2021 were Australia, Saudi Arabia, Spain, the USA, Mongolia and Oman, all with levels greater than 14.5 tonnes of CO2 per capita. The orthographic projection depicts that the continents of Africa and South America created a relatively low amount of CO2 emissions from fossil fuels per capita when compared to those of North America, Asia, and Oceania. Emission trends are further explored in the following bubble plot; select different years from the dropdown box to visualise global changes in CO2 emission production over time.

In the year 1980, the USA produced the largest amount of CO2 emissions in the world with approximately 4,800 Mt. North America also dominated other metrics with Sint Maarten producing 85.11 tonnes of CO2 per capita and Curacao possessing the highest emissions per unit of land area, with 30,647 t/km^2.

Fast forward to 2021; China overtook the USA to produce the the highest amount of total CO2 emissions with a value of 11,472 Mt, Qatar holds the global emissions density high with 35.5 tonnes per capita, and Jordan produced the largest amount of CO2 per unit of land area with 213,281 t/km^2. This highlights the extend to which Asia's CO2 emissions outgrew those of North America from 1980 to 2021, to become the continent with the largest contribution to CO2 emissions around the world.

Quantifying Change Over Time

The following charts depict the composition of the different sources of CO2 emissions over time, and the percentage change in global emissions and population from 1980 to 2021 by continent. Select each source in the CO2 Emission Sources area chart legend to highlight their relative contribution to global emissions.

The CO2 Emission Sources area chart shows that Coal and Oil comprise the majority of total emissions from 1980 to 2021. Coal emissions grew from 7024 Mt in 1980 to 14976 Mt in 2021, while Oil emissions grew from 8499 Mt to 10802 Mt. Coal surpassed Oil as the largest single CO2 emissions source in the world between the years 2000 and 2010. The amount of emissions from all of the listed sources increased over the specified time period.

The Growth Over Time bar chart compares trends of emissions and population growth by continent. Asia's CO2 emissions growth greatly exceeded its population growth, with emissions increasing by approximately 48,000 % from 1980 to 2021, and population increasing by 7,500 %. Africa had the second highest emissions growth and the highest population growth. All other continents' emissions growth was greater than their population growth except for Europe, which actually saw a 282.3 % decrease in the total CO2 emissions between 1980 and 2021.

What About Renewable Energy?

Hover over the Global Electricity Generation Sources line chart to see the amount of electricity generated from each source (fossil fuels, nuclear, renewables) for each year between 2000 and 2020. Since the turn of the century, fossil fuels have dominated electricity production, and continued to rise steadily to a peak value of 14,886 TWh in 2018 before slightly dropping off to 14,369 TWh in 2020. The next largest source of electricity generated is from renewables such as wind, solar, and hydro. Starting at just over 2,000 TWh in 2000, the contribution of renewables to global electricity production has increased at steadily increasing rate, as indicated by the rising slope of the Renewables line on the chart, to a value of 6,971 TWh in 2020. This indicates the world's growing reliance on using renewable sources to generate electricity. Nuclear energy production has remained around the 2,000 TWh mark during year from 2000 to 2020.
